# Introduction to Science

### What is Science?

One of the initial misconceptions about science is the assumption baked into the question "What is science?"

The assumption is that people think about science as some real unified natural thing, it isn't. And, as we'll see next, the demarcation problem is what happens when you take that assumption too seriously and try to define the borders of something that can't be categorized.

#### Using Science as a Label

Science is given the reification treatment (i.e., treating science, which is a loose abstract thing, as a concrete natural thing). It shows in how people talk about science, when people say "science says," "according to science," "scientists say" "science proves," "science tells us," "science shows us" and "science has found." ~Talking about science in this way could be explained away in quietist terms: when we say science, we aren't suggesting it is a unified natural thing, but refering to what science entails. This position would be rare though, and the reification problem is what most commonly appears in how we think about science.~

So, when we ask, "What is science?" and drop the assumption that we mean it to be a natural kind of thing, we get a practical way of trying to answer this by looking at what scientists do, and attempting to define science through instances, rather than through abstractions. Without this specific type of analysis, science becomes a polysemous word and retains the catch-all semantic marker used to invoke epistemic authority, rather than ground claims in field-specific territory.

For instance, rather than reading a Nature article on a new type of medicine and saying "Science discovers new miracle drug," the field-specific way to ground the claim would refer to the field itself and their specific groundwork, "Pharmacologists confirm new drug cures X in trials."

One caveat to note, and will be expressed in the next section, is the contigent nature of attempting to define science. Although reifying science is argubly more problematic, attempting to define science through particular instances runs into issues too. For instance, instances are temporal and historically contigent. Defining science based off what scientists were doing in 1910 would be introduce the issue of what epistemic status they had access too, which was less than ours in certain regards, and also, how science as an institution was arranged during their time.

So, the criteria used to define science is a slipperly and temporal thing, and will continue to be revised as technology improves, institutions change, epistemic access widens, and how future practioners within different fields of science tackle their prior commitments to shape their field's methods. 

So then, when is it appropriate to talk about science? There is a distinct flavor of science that has to be specified, which is _science as an epistemic practice._ This type of flavor is the one that attempts to flatten the diversity of field-specific epistemic groundwork into one pancake-image called _science as a method._

In our current time, it's appropriate to talk about science as a collective of institutions and their respective journals, labs, funding bodies, credentialing systems but without conflating each fields' specific peer review process and contesting epistemic traditions as belong to a single method or set of norms/values. Though, this isn't a static definition, or committing to a platonic view of science, rather, as historically contigent and subject to reorganization as these charactetistics change. Even now, credientialing systems, pedagogy, and what counts as a legitimate journal is shifting.

How we talk about science will continue to be tracking what's currently configured, and who belongs to the collective. Scientists then, are not practioners of some scientific method, but as people belonging to this diverse system occupying genuinely diverse and niche roles. And how scientists talk about science will be eternally important for public trust.

Science is not a _thing_ insofar as reification goes. Ironically, reifying science makes _science_ a label, rather than a category. As a label, _science_ becomes a catch-all semantic marker that points to a polysemous _kind_ of science. More than anything, reifying science makes it suitable as a credibility token than as a category.

As a category, _science_ is particularly difficult to define. The demarcation problem was a 20th century attempt to cleanly draw those lines between what _science_ is from _non-science_ or _psuedo-science._ Still, it's likely best to stop thinking of science as one thing, and to turn the question to, what instances of science can we observe?

#### Instantiating Science

Instantiating science comes with a lot set of issues—a set I'd rather not waste time on. The main thing that comes to mind for many, is the central throughline that unifies all of science. This is the demarcation problem in a different coat. There has been many attempts to describe what specific meta-criterion instantiates science. The pedagogy in philosophy of science tends towards the canonical start of logical empiricism, and then the gradual revisions like Popper's falsification, Kuhn's paradigm shifts, Lakatos' research programmes, Quines' web of belief, and Feyerabend "anything goes."

Each has their own specific history, rich in detail. I'm hand-waving away the nuance of a historical period as I'm still in the process of reading. The main point is, the search for a centralized throughline that can be captured by definitive instianiations of science is a contentious and didn't yield any one method. In fact, in _Against Methods,_ the real final say seems to have been Feyerabend's "anything goes."

### The Scientific Method

Many would retort: "Science is unified by the scientific method." Except, the scientific method assumes one central method that every field of science performs. This couldn't be further from the truth if we simply consider how chemistry is different ecology. In chemistry, you work within defined spaces where everything can be accounted for. In ecology, you work in an environment where you have to relinquish most everything, and decide what's worth accounting for. These are simply derivitations of a single scientific method, these are incommensurable approaches to generating knowledge about a field.

Knowledge becomes one of the first stopping points for scientific thinking. Specifically, how chemistry and ecology have different epistemic standards for what type of method is entailed in their respective disciplines. Moreso, neither could fully rely on a singular field-specific scientific method alone: each requires a plethora of methods. This would mean that each field has their own standard of methodological pluralism. Well, what informs their respective pluralism?

### Axiology and Epistemology

Scientific methodological pluralism is recursive. Fields require new and critical ways to approach new field-specific problems constantly. There is a common trope that science makes an empirical observation, then proposes a hypothesis, logically deduces what it expects to find through prediction, then tests those conditions and compares what was hypothesized to what was found. This is the standard hypothetico-deductive method, and as before, doesn't work in a plethora of fields. Instead, each field has to propose unique ways to generate reliable knowledge, and that type of thinking requires certain values and epistemic practices/norms/standards/goals, which are shaped by prior commitments about what knowledge is for, to inform those methods.

Fields have to consider what their methods are attempting to capture, and how they'll choose to interpret them. Before scientists in a field selects methods, they have to preselect the criteria for what would count as a successful outcome and those criteria aren't methodological. Instead, they would be prior to method, like epistemic metacriteria or goals. These epistemic goals aren't neutral, or objective. They're constrained by how the field designates what knowledge is for, who it serves, what makes it reliable, how to interpret it.

For instance, scientists who treat the subject matter of their field's epistemology as law-governed and determistic will set different epistemic goals than another that is probablistic or historically contigent. A contemporary example is p-value in statistics, with one of the questions currently contested being: is p > 0.05 reasonable, or should a new convention be set? The question seems methodological on the surface, but requires axiological commitments to explore the epistemological questions: what counts as explanation good enough to act on, or how much precision is required before a result is publishable.

Oftem, these are not a round table discussions, but a bureaucratic process of peer review and revision. Often, it comes with intellectual clashes and refinement. Professionals are forced to get technical and stipulate what they're assuming for the sake of proceeding, and what they're certain they can support with evidence. At one point, there was a movement to suggest that anything that wasn't technical was meaningless: that is, only scientific claims had value, and everything else was subsidiary and meaningless. This was the logical positivism era, and it forces scientists and scientific thinkers to get analytic about their claims.

Insofar as getting technical, it was successful but has captured the western world in an anglophone tradition that treats science primarily about truth-tracking. It signals that science is primarily an epistemic enterprise that happens to be practiced by people. And so, one of the important questions this raises is: is this the right way to demarcate science? Proposing that exact sciences like logic, mathematics, or something non-exact like philosophy be integrated as fields of science might reveal that boundaries are constructed, rather than discovered.

If there is a possibility that boundaries are constructed, then there is a case that the knowledge produced by different fields of knowledge is informed by a certain epistemic tradition. This tradition will shape what knowledge is for, who it serves, and the types of interpretive choices made along the way.

### Metaphysics and Ontology

Outside of philosophy of science, there is a little known distinction between methodological naturalism and ontological/metaphysical naturalism. This distinct isn't just the words, but the core claim about what science is. In methodological naturalism, the claim is that the natural world is observable and offers naturalistic explanation without requiring supernatural or other-worldly explanations or ontological commitments. It focuses mostly on scientific practice and process, without commiting to any ontological basis for the method. On the other hand, making harder claims such as mind-independent reality is a deeper metaphysical assertion that remains unfalsifiable.

Often, naturalistic commitments are inferred rather than baselessly asserted as axiomatic truth. However, some take naturalistic claims further in materialist or physicalist commitments. When these lines get blurry, people get confused.

# Introduction to Science

### What is Science?

One of the initial misconceptions about science is asking, "What is science?" Another sentiment is asserting, "This is what the science says [...]." Science is not a _thing_ insofar as reification goes. Ironically, reifying science makes _science_ a label, rather than a category. As a label, _science_ becomes a catch-all semantic marker that points to a polysemous _kind_ of science. More than anything, reifying science makes it suitable as a credibility token than as a category.

As a category, _science_ is particularly difficult to define. The demarcation problem was a 20th century attempt to cleanly draw those lines between what _science_ is from _non-science_ or _psuedo-science._ Still, it's likely best to stop thinking of science as one thing, and to turn the question to, what instances of science can we observe?

#### Instantiating Science

Instantiating science comes with a lot set of issues—a set I'd rather not waste time on. The main thing that comes to mind for many, is the central throughline that unifies all of science. This is the demarcation problem in a different coat. There has been many attempts to describe what specific meta-criterion instantiates science. The pedagogy in philosophy of science tends towards the canonical start of logical empiricism, and then the gradual revisions like Popper's falsification, Kuhn's paradigm shifts, Lakatos' research programmes, Quines' web of belief, and Feyerabend "anything goes."

Each has their own specific history, rich in detail. I'm hand-waving away the nuance of a historical period as I'm still in the process of reading. The main point is, the search for a centralized throughline that can be captured by definitive instianiations of science is a contentious and didn't yield any one method. In fact, in _Against Methods,_ the real final say seems to have been Feyerabend's "anything goes."

### The Scientific Method

Many would retort: "Science is unified by the scientific method." Except, the scientific method assumes one central method that every field of science performs. This couldn't be further from the truth if we simply consider how chemistry is different ecology. In chemistry, you work within defined spaces where everything can be accounted for. In ecology, you work in an environment where you have to relinquish most everything, and decide what's worth accounting for. These are simply derivitations of a single scientific method, these are incommensurable approaches to generating knowledge about a field.

Knowledge becomes one of the first stopping points for scientific thinking. Specifically, how chemistry and ecology have different epistemic standards for what type of method is entailed in their respective disciplines. Moreso, neither could fully rely on a singular field-specific scientific method alone: each requires a plethora of methods. This would mean that each field has their own standard of methodological pluralism. Well, what informs their respective pluralism?

### Axiology and Epistemology

Scientific methodological pluralism is recursive. Fields require new and critical ways to approach new field-specific problems constantly. There is a common trope that science makes an empirical observation, then proposes a hypothesis, logically deduces what it expects to find through prediction, then tests those conditions and compares what was hypothesized to what was found. This is the standard hypothetico-deductive method, and as before, doesn't work in a plethora of fields. Instead, each field has to propose unique ways to generate reliable knowledge, and that type of thinking requires certain values and epistemic practices/norms/standards to inform those methods.

Fields have to consider what their methods are attempting to capture, and how they'll choose to interpret them. This is not a round table discussion, but a process of peer review and revision. Often, it comes with intellectual clashes and refinement. Professionals are forced to get technical and stipulate what they're assuming for the sake of proceeding, and what they're certain they can support with evidence. At one point, there was a movement to suggest that anything that wasn't technical was meaningless: that is, only scientific claims had value, and everything else was subsidiary and meaningless. This was the logical positivism era, and it forces scientists and scientific thinkers to get analytic about their claims.

The main critique against anything non-empirical was that it was metaphysical.

### Metaphysics and Ontology

Outside of philosophy of science, there is a little known distinction between methodological naturalism and ontological/metaphysical naturalism. This distinct isn't just the words, but the core claim about what science is. In methodological naturalism, the claim is that the natural world is observable and offers naturalistic explanation without requiring supernatural or other-worldly explanations or ontological commitments. It focuses mostly on scientific practice and process, without commiting to any ontological basis for the method. On the other hand, making harder claims such as mind-independent reality is a deeper metaphysical assertion that remains unfalsifiable.

Often, naturalistic commitments are inferred rather than baselessly asserted as axiomatic truth. However, some take naturalistic claims further in materialist or physicalist commitments. When these lines get blurry, people get confused.

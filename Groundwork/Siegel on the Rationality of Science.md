# Introduction
Brian S. Baigrie critques Harvey Siegel (1985) in his "Siegel on the Rationality of Science." Essentially, Baigrie is pointing out that Siegel's project to place science in the a priori space using: "a general commitment to evidence." Baigrie is primarily concerned with the "unwelcome consequences" of this project; primarily that it's too permissive and doesn't cleanly individuate scientific belief from any other type of belief.

Baigrie takes Siegel's project, critiques it using the exact instances that Siegel dismisses, and explicitly drags the demarcation problem back over to empirical grounds. Siegel's claim is that the instrumental view of science offered by Laudan, is that scientific belief should be evaluated through rationality; as opposed to whether a method produces a good outcome.

At the surface, Siegel is correct. Simply dropping necessary a priori commitments in place of a dedication to good outcomes (e.g., "fruits of such inquiry") does seem malformed. During this time, philosophers of science talked about claims, hypotheses, and theories as mind-independent propositionally true entities. And so, the idea in the instrumentalist view was that these did not need to be evaluated rationally if they fit the instrumental analysis.

However, Baigrie is critical of Siegel's "provocative" thesis: instrumental science isn't sufficient, rationality is necessary. However, it isn't outright dismissal of Siegel's observation, it's a critique of Siegel's solution: "apriostic commitment to evidence." Baigrie writes,
> If Siegel is right in contending that an account of the rationality of science should address the rationality of scientific beliefs as well as the rationality of scientific inquiry, the question for Siegel is whether his aprioristic rendering of science's rationality allows us to draw a line between beliefs that are worth of acceptance and others that are not.

So, Baigrie agrees with that rationality is worth consideration, but questions Siegel's move for being too permissive. Baigrie writes, "Does it furnish a line between genuine scientific beliefs and psuedoscientific ones?" This is the crux of the whole paper, and the major theme Baigrie will hammer home. And so, Baigrie sets out to steelman the position and then pressure test it.

One of the distinctions Baigrie makes pertains to the Scientific Method (SM), which he states Siegel rejects as being methodological, and rather, epistemic. Serving to evaluate the justification of scientific subject matter like theories, rather than being mistaken as a singular method that pretends science doesn't require methodological plurality.

The main point that Siegel is making, is that the SM is not a statically bound generalization for science. Instead, he's correctly arguing that if a SM exists, then it's necessary that it consists of general characterizations that are informed contigent on the period science exists in. The example he provides demonstrates this cleanly: the general principles are [explanatory adequacy] + [insistence on testing] + [commitment to inductive support] but general principle of the SM depends on the scientific period in question (however adequacy is conceived) + (however testing is to be best done) + (however inductive inference is through to be best made).

Siegel is correctly identifying that any postualated SM must contain timeless general principles like those bracketed, of which, serve as placeholders to survive any "periods of scientific upheaval," by remaining contigently dependent on the time period in question, with those in parentheses.

However, Baigrie is pushing back on the single overreach Siegel makes when he attempts to ground the SM in "a commitment to evidence." The reason Siegel is making this move is because, despite his ingenuity to see that the SM would require a specific epistemic structure to survive "scientific upheaval", it still lacks a shared criterion that makes it necessary at all. And so, rather than allowing it to be purely rational, without a necessary condition; it remains instrumental, and falls into the same category he's attempting to escape. So, Siegel grounds that "specific methodological criteria of science," in _"a commitment to evidence."_

This is the specific point that Baigrie chews on. He quotes Siegel:
> ... so as to establish the evidentiary support of hypotheses and theories. The hallmark of SM is its commitment to the establishment of the epistemic worth of scientific claims by virtue of the establishment of the evidential support of those claims.

Immediately after designating the need for a commitment to evidentiary support, Baigrie correctly puts the questions directly on what makes this commitment _"scientific"_ to begin with. It's the classical move of undermining the position by asking whether it's sawing off the branch it sits on. In my mind, it's a clean move when it works, but a lazy one. However, Baigrie doesn't end his critique here, he begs the question as to whether this commitment is even necessarily science-specific, saying, "Science is surely not the only discipline that establishes the worthiness of hypotheses on the basis of evidence."

### An Aside
I'm disconnecting this new thought from the analysis of the essay, because I'm going to invoke the presentism fallacy to critique a sub-point in this essay. Immediately, after this quote, he lists 3 examples: "engineering, the courts, the social sciences," which highlights a bidirectional historical pull between science expanding it's boundaries (encorachment) and also fields that were not previously considered scientific attempting to get technical to purchase membership and gain the credibility of belonging to science.

This framing has a lot of issues, and at a surface level, it's definitely inaccurate. However, the deeper sentiment I wanted to point out was what is being implied here: that scientism in the last half-century has encroached on fields, but fields also wanted the _science_ social credibility token.

---

Baigrie even quotes Siegel making this point directly, "For I am not claiming that there is anything about SM which is unique to science in the sense that only science does or can do that unique thing." However, Siegel is making a specific point that individuates this the SM from other disciplines, he says, "science typically, or at least ideally, manifests and organizes its affairs with specific attention to the commitment to evidence." Siegel is not wrong, he's individuating that science does orient itself toward a commitment to evidence, an observation about science that has aged well. Baigrie doesn't necessarily disagree either, he restates Siegel's conclusion cleanly: while others _may_, science universally _does_.

Before getting into the next point, it's worth recalling that Baigrie does not disagree with Siegel's project: ground science aprioristically to discredit science as purely instrumental analysis. Baigrie is arguing something more specific: that Siegel is too permissive with the necessary a priori condition of the SM being: _a comittment to evidence._

And so, Baigrie quotes Siegel conceding that he recognizes not all intellectual pursuits require their beliefs and justifications to be evidentiary. Mainly, as Baigrie states, Siegel is pointing to psuedosciences and folk belief systems. And more critically, he shows that Siegel charges these with inerrancy and insularity when he says "are held in spite of, or systematically protected from, contrary evidence." And the charge that Baigrie delivers is: are you truly drawing the line between science and psuedoscience on the basis of evidentiary norms?
